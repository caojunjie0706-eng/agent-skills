# Document Writing Method

## 1. Start From Expectation And Goal

Before writing, answer:

- Why is this document needed?
- Who will read it?
- Why do they care?
- What should they decide, believe, or do after reading?

Common failure mode: the surface request is not the real expectation. "Analyze user profile" may mean "prove the business understands its users and has a plan"; "NPS report" may mean "identify the business problem and solution system," not "repeat the score."

## 2. Demand Perspective Before Supply Perspective

Demand perspective asks what readers need:

- Decision makers: whole business state, strategic focus, investment decisions, key risks, and solutions.
- Collaborators: what affects their work, what they need to coordinate, where they are blocked.
- FYI readers: compressed business situation and context.

Supply perspective lists what the producing group did. Use it for operating reviews and audits, but avoid it for executive or cross-functional materials where readers do not share the org structure.

## 3. Story Line: Big Environment, Small Environment, Goal

For substantial docs, write a 300-400 character story line first.

- Big environment: what the audience cares about, what consensus already exists, and what lacks consensus.
- Small environment: what the writer sees from their vantage point, which stakeholders are involved, and what conflicts or gaps exist.
- Goal: what the story should influence in the environment.

A good story line gives many readers the same reasoning path. It does not just state the conclusion; it leads readers to the conclusion.

## 4. Influence Decision Logic

For complex problems, the writer may not be the decision-maker. The document's job is to clarify decision logic:

- Who decides?
- What options exist?
- What facts and tradeoffs matter?
- What happens under each choice?
- What support or alignment is required?

Avoid documents with only one solution and no decision criteria. They are hard to evaluate and easy to ignore.

## 5. Business Breakdown As Evidence, Not Main Plot

Business logic decomposition creates the evidence library. It is not automatically the story.

Use evidence selectively:

- Data and growth formulas explain drivers and blockers.
- User research explains behavior and motivation, but may be biased.
- Cases make abstract issues concrete.
- Historical examples show known patterns or constraints. Use generalized examples for public or reusable documents unless the source material is explicitly approved for sharing.

Start with the viewpoint, then choose evidence that supports, challenges, or refines it.

## 6. The 2.5-Dimension Business Frame

Use this for business review and reporting materials:

- Dimension 1: overall state. What are the most important metrics and progress if the reader only has 30-60 seconds?
- Dimension 2: breakdown. Decompose growth, decline, or quality into the most explanatory dimensions. Choose the cut that best reveals the true problem.
- Dimension 2.5: deep dive. Pick one important case or topic and unpack it to reveal a system-level issue, risk, or action.

Avoid excessive second- and third-level breakdowns in broad-audience or executive materials. Deep execution detail often reads as "local work" rather than a shared business issue.

## 7. Recurring Update Materials

Recurring update materials are not work logs. Each module should include:

- Core metrics and week-over-week trend.
- Cause and interpretation, not just numbers.
- Product, program, or workstream actions and expected impact.
- Risks, delays, dependency bottlenecks, disagreements, or decision points.
- Responsible party or stakeholder mentions where support is needed.

Do not include smooth progress with no risk, decision, or learning unless it is critical context.

## 8. Key Problem And Consensus Documents

Use this process:

1. Analyze the problem: who raised it, why, who is related, what answer each party needs.
2. Confirm the frame: form the story line and section outline.
3. Add evidence: explain the core viewpoints with data, cases, research, and history.
4. Confirm viewpoints: review whether subpoints support the main point and whether relationships are parallel, progressive, or cause-effect.
5. Produce consensus: convert aligned viewpoints into principles, operating rules, and best practices.

The target output is not just analysis; it is a shared principle and guidance that can shape future work.

## 9. Expression Rules

- Write viewpoint headings, not neutral labels.
- Put conclusions first.
- Quantify where possible. Prefer "A improves x by 1%" over "A is significantly better."
- Compress aggressively. Remove content that does not support the point.
- Use plain language. Avoid unexplained abbreviations and professional jargon.
- Use charts only when they reduce cognitive load. Tables are best for comparison; diagrams are best for relationships. Each visual should tell one story.
- Before publishing reusable or public material, remove company names, personal names, internal links, exact confidential metrics, proprietary project names, customer-identifying details, and access paths.

## 10. Review Criteria

Review a document against these questions:

- Is the target reader clear?
- Is the expected decision or action clear?
- Can the reader understand the main view from the headings?
- Does the story line explain why this matters now?
- Is the business broken down by the most revealing dimension?
- Are claims supported by evidence, and are data/user-research biases considered?
- Are risks, conflicts, decision points, responsible parties, and support needs explicit?
- Can 20-30% of the words be removed without losing meaning?
